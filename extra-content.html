Example: Effect Size Interpretation

When evaluating education research involving effect sizes, we use guidance from Kraft (2019), which provides empirically grounded benchmarks based on 1,942 effect sizes from 747 RCTs of education interventions. Cohen’s conventional benchmarks (small = 0.2, medium = 0.5, large = 0.8) were derived from small lab studies and are far too large relative to what field-based education interventions actually produce. Kraft’s adjusted benchmarks are:

Category Threshold
Small < 0.05 SD
Medium 0.05–0.19 SD
Large ≥ 0.20 SD

The median effect size across education RCTs is 0.10 SD. Large-scale, pre-registered RCTs produce medians as low as 0.03 SD.

When asking AI to help interpret or contextualize effect sizes, include this framing in your prompt so the AI applies these benchmarks rather than Cohen’s conventions.

System prompt for effect size work (use in a Claude Project or paste at the start of a conversation):

When evaluating education-related research involving effect sizes, 
use Kraft (2019) benchmarks rather than Cohen's conventions: 
small < 0.05 SD, medium 0.05–0.19 SD, large ≥ 0.20 SD. The median 
effect size across education RCTs is 0.10 SD. Note the following 
when contextualizing effects: (1) whether the design is causal or 
correlational, (2) whether the outcome is proximal or distal to the 
intervention, (3) design features that may inflate effect sizes 
(small/targeted samples, researcher-designed measures, TOT vs. ITT), 
and (4) cost and scalability considerations.

Interpreting an effect size:

I am reviewing a study of an SEL intervention that reports a 
Cohen's d of 0.18 on a teacher-reported SEL measure administered 
immediately post-intervention. The study used a randomized design 
with N=280 students in grades 3-5. Using Kraft (2019) benchmarks, 
how should I interpret this effect size? What contextual factors 
are relevant?

Example: Exploring a literature

I am designing a study on [topic]. Before I finalize the design, 
what are the key bodies of empirical literature I should be 
familiar with? What are the main debates or unresolved questions 
in this area? Please be specific about subfields and landmark 
papers where possible — I will verify and read the primary sources.

Stress-testing a research design:

Here is my proposed research design: [describe design]. Before 
I finalize it, what are the strongest methodological objections 
someone could raise? What threats to validity am I most exposed 
to? What would a skeptical reviewer say about this approach?

Measurement Development

For some projects, it can be useful to use a dedicated Claude Project (or Cowork session) with a system prompt and an attached reference document. This keeps the AI grounded in established psychometric evidence rather than generating advice from general knowledge alone.

The reference document is scale_best_practices.docx, which lives in the lab’s shared Google Drive. This document was built from primary scientific literature by psychometric experts — Krosnick & Presser (2010), Preston & Colman (2000), Saris et al. (2010), Schaeffer & Dykema (2020), Ward & Meade (2023), and others. The document was not generated by AI. AI was used as a thought partner during its development — asked to identify bodies of work and considerations that might be missing — but all editorial decisions about what to include were made by Mike. The result is a reference grounded in the actual scientific literature, not in AI’s general impressions of measurement best practices.

This is a concrete example of the thought partner approach: you drive the intellectual work, use AI to surface potential blind spots, and exercise your own judgment about what belongs. The AI does not write the document — it helps you ask better questions about what should be in it.

System prompt for the Claude Project:

This involves measurement issues related to other research projects. 
Take the role of an expert in measurement development. Use the 
document on best practices to guide but feel free to go beyond this.

Attach scale_best_practices.docx to the project before beginning.
