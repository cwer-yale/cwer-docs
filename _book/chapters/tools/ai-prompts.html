8  Working with AI

This chapter describes how CWER uses AI tools across three areas of research work: idea generation, reviewing and editing, and data management and analysis. Each section includes the prompts and setups we use in practice.

8.1 About Our AI Tools

CWER uses Claude (by Anthropic) as its primary AI tool. We chose Claude for two reasons: its strong performance on research and analytical tasks, and Anthropic’s serious attention to the ethical development of AI. Gemini (by Google) is our secondary recommendation for the same reasons — strong performance combined with a developer that takes responsible AI seriously. Both are available through Yale’s Clarity platform (see below).

We do not have a blanket recommendation for other tools, though the landscape is evolving quickly. When in doubt, ask Mike or Joanna before using a new AI tool for anything related to lab work.

8.2 Data Security and AI

Yale’s Clarity platform (clarity.yale.edu) is the only platform approved for working with identifiable or sensitive research data. Clarity provides secure, encrypted connections and assures that Yale data will not be used to train AI models. It supports Claude, GPT-4o, and Gemini, and is appropriate for most high-risk data with the exception of electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI). If your work involves ePHI or data use agreements with external partners, check with Mike or Joanna before using any AI tool — including Clarity.

When using Claude.ai, Gemini, or any other external AI tool outside of Clarity, never paste real participant data. Use anonymized or synthetic examples instead.

8.3 General Prompting Principles

Getting useful output from AI requires clear, specific input. A few principles that apply across all tasks:

Give context first. AI tools have no memory of your project unless you tell them. Before asking for help, describe what you are working on, what software you are using, what you have already tried, and what the output should look like.

Be specific about what you want. “Help me clean this data” is too vague. “I have a CSV with 342 rows and 45 variables. I need to recode variables coded -99 and -98 to NA, reverse score items 3, 7, and 12, and compute a mean composite score across items 1-10, excluding cases with more than 20% missing. Write R code using tidyverse.” gives the AI something concrete to work with.

Treat output as a first draft. Review every piece of AI-generated code before running it. Check that it does what you intended. AI output can be subtly wrong in ways that do not throw errors — always verify results against expectations.

Use AI as a thought partner, not an oracle. Some of the most valuable uses of AI are not asking it to produce something, but asking it to think alongside you — to surface considerations you may have missed, identify gaps in a plan, or present the argument against an approach you are leaning toward. The measurement development prompt below is a concrete example of this in practice.

Note AI contributions. Document AI assistance in your script headers and, where appropriate, in manuscript methods sections. This reflects our commitment to research transparency described in the Being a Team Member chapter.

8.4 Writing Assistance with Grammarly

Grammarly is an AI-powered writing assistant that checks grammar, spelling, punctuation, clarity, and style in real time. The recommended version for CWER team members is the Grammarly desktop app, which works seamlessly across all the applications you already use — Word, Outlook, email clients, web browsers, and desktop applications — without any copying and pasting. Suggestions appear inline wherever you are writing.

For CWER work, Grammarly is most useful for:

  • Polishing manuscript drafts, reports, and partner communications before sharing
  • Catching typos and grammatical errors in research reports and grant proposals
  • Improving sentence clarity and concision in methods and results sections
  • Checking team member writing during the review process

8.4.1 Setup

Download the Grammarly desktop app for Mac or Windows at grammarly.com/desktop. Installation is straightforward — once installed, Grammarly works automatically in the background across your applications. The free version covers grammar, spelling, and punctuation. Grammarly Pro adds clarity, tone, and style suggestions, which are useful for academic writing.

8.4.2 A Note on Data Privacy

Do not use Grammarly with text containing sensitive research data or participant information. Grammarly processes text on external servers. For sensitive content, use Microsoft Word’s built-in Editor (Review → Editor) instead, which provides grammar and style checking without sending text to external servers. You can also temporarily turn Grammarly off for a specific application using the Grammarly desktop app settings.

8.5 Idea Generation

AI is well-suited for early-stage thinking — exploring a literature you are less familiar with, stress-testing a research design, or identifying considerations you may have overlooked. The key is to treat AI output as a starting point that you verify and refine, not as a definitive answer.

8.5.1 Effect Size Interpretation

When evaluating education research involving effect sizes, we use guidance from Kraft (2019), which provides empirically grounded benchmarks based on 1,942 effect sizes from 747 RCTs of education interventions. Cohen’s conventional benchmarks (small = 0.2, medium = 0.5, large = 0.8) were derived from small lab studies and are far too large relative to what field-based education interventions actually produce. Kraft’s adjusted benchmarks are:

Category Threshold
Small < 0.05 SD
Medium 0.05–0.19 SD
Large ≥ 0.20 SD

The median effect size across education RCTs is 0.10 SD. Large-scale, pre-registered RCTs produce medians as low as 0.03 SD.

When asking AI to help interpret or contextualize effect sizes, include this framing in your prompt so the AI applies these benchmarks rather than Cohen’s conventions.

System prompt for effect size work (use in a Claude Project or paste at the start of a conversation):

When evaluating education-related research involving effect sizes, 
use Kraft (2019) benchmarks rather than Cohen's conventions: 
small < 0.05 SD, medium 0.05–0.19 SD, large ≥ 0.20 SD. The median 
effect size across education RCTs is 0.10 SD. Note the following 
when contextualizing effects: (1) whether the design is causal or 
correlational, (2) whether the outcome is proximal or distal to the 
intervention, (3) design features that may inflate effect sizes 
(small/targeted samples, researcher-designed measures, TOT vs. ITT), 
and (4) cost and scalability considerations.

Interpreting an effect size:

I am reviewing a study of an SEL intervention that reports a 
Cohen's d of 0.18 on a teacher-reported SEL measure administered 
immediately post-intervention. The study used a randomized design 
with N=280 students in grades 3-5. Using Kraft (2019) benchmarks, 
how should I interpret this effect size? What contextual factors 
are relevant?

Exploring a literature:

I am designing a study on [topic]. Before I finalize the design, 
what are the key bodies of empirical literature I should be 
familiar with? What are the main debates or unresolved questions 
in this area? Please be specific about subfields and landmark 
papers where possible — I will verify and read the primary sources.

Stress-testing a research design:

Here is my proposed research design: [describe design]. Before 
I finalize it, what are the strongest methodological objections 
someone could raise? What threats to validity am I most exposed 
to? What would a skeptical reviewer say about this approach?

8.5.2 Measurement Development

For measurement development work, we use a dedicated Claude Project (or Cowork session) with a system prompt and an attached reference document. This keeps the AI grounded in established psychometric evidence rather than generating advice from general knowledge alone.

The reference document is scale_best_practices.docx, which lives in the lab’s shared Google Drive. This document was built from primary scientific literature by psychometric experts — Krosnick & Presser (2010), Preston & Colman (2000), Saris et al. (2010), Schaeffer & Dykema (2020), Ward & Meade (2023), and others. The document was not generated by AI. AI was used as a thought partner during its development — asked to identify bodies of work and considerations that might be missing — but all editorial decisions about what to include were made by Mike. The result is a reference grounded in the actual scientific literature, not in AI’s general impressions of measurement best practices.

This is a concrete example of the thought partner approach: you drive the intellectual work, use AI to surface potential blind spots, and exercise your own judgment about what belongs. The AI does not write the document — it helps you ask better questions about what should be in it.

System prompt for the Claude Project:

This involves measurement issues related to other research projects. 
Take the role of an expert in measurement development. Use the 
document on best practices to guide but feel free to go beyond this.

Attach scale_best_practices.docx to the project before beginning.

Designing a new scale:

I am developing a teacher-report measure of student self-management 
for use in grades K-3. The construct includes behavioral regulation, 
emotional control, and goal-directed persistence. I plan to use a 
5-point response scale. Based on the best practices document, what 
response format and label set would you recommend? Are there any 
considerations specific to teacher-report measures I should be 
aware of?

Reviewing existing items:

Here are 8 draft items for a social awareness subscale. Please 
review them against the best practices document and flag any 
concerns about item wording, response format match, potential 
acquiescence bias, or cognitive burden. Suggest revisions where 
needed.

[paste items here]

Using AI as a thought partner on scale design:

I have made the following decisions about my scale design: 
[describe decisions]. Before I finalize, what considerations or 
tradeoffs might I be overlooking? Are there bodies of psychometric 
literature I should consult that the best practices document does 
not cover?

8.6 Reviewing & Editing

When asking AI to review written work, the most useful output comes from prompts that specify the review criteria clearly and explicitly discourage flattery. The following system prompt is used for reviewing research writing in CWER. It can be pasted at the start of a conversation or set as a system prompt in a Claude Project.

System prompt for reviewing research writing:

Content in here concerns research projects.

When I ask you to review something I've written, check to make 
sure of the following:
1. Text is in APA format unless requested otherwise.
2. The writing is clear, as free of jargon as possible, and concise.
3. Logical arguments are sound.
4. There are no statements that are weakly argued or lack 
   justification. Arguments should not overstate or understate 
   the evidence.
5. If I'm critiquing someone's work, make sure I'm doing so 
   charitably and accurately.

Do not flatter me when reviewing my work. Be constructively 
critical and put yourself in the role of a reviewer at a 
scientific journal who is aiming to improve the work through 
strong critique.

Reviewing a manuscript section:

Please review the following [methods / results / discussion] 
section using the criteria in your instructions. Identify the 
most important issues first, then secondary issues. Be specific 
about what to fix and why.

[paste text here]

Verifying a reference:

Please verify that the following reference is real and provide 
a correctly formatted APA 7th edition citation and a sample 
parenthetical in-text citation I can use directly in a manuscript.

[paste reference here]

Checking for misreporting:

Here are my analysis results and the corresponding text from my 
results section. Please check for any discrepancies between the 
numbers in the text and the numbers in the output — wrong values, 
rounding errors, or results described in the wrong direction.

Results output: [paste output]
Results text: [paste text]

Drafting a methods section:

I ran a multilevel model in R using lme4. Here are the details: 
[describe model]. Write a methods section paragraph describing 
the analytic approach in APA style, appropriate for a journal 
manuscript. Do not invent any details — only use what I have 
provided.
