8  AI Tools

This chapter describes how we use AI tools for idea generation and reviewing and editing. See the Data Management and Statistical Analysis sections for using AI for those types of tasks.

8.1 Recommended AI Tools

8.1.1 General Purpose LLMs: Claude & Gemini

Claude (Anthropic) and Gemini (Google) are large language model assistants useful at multiple stages of the research process. We chose Claude for two reasons: its strong performance on research and analytical tasks, and Anthropic’s serious attention to the ethical development of AI. Gemini (by Google) is our secondary recommendation for the same reasons. Both are highly capable for tasks where the researcher supplies the content and the AI assists with processing, drafting, or reasoning.

However, neither Claude nor Gemini should be trusted to retrieve citations, report statistics, or make factual claims about the literature without verification. Use Consensus (see below), Google Scholar, or a traditional database search for evidence retrieval. Use Claude and Gemini for everything that surrounds that evidence such as, writing, coding, organizing, and reasoning.

Claude is particularly useful for:

  • Long document analysis — reading and synthesizing lengthy manuscripts, reports, grant applications, or literature with a large context window
  • Writing and editing — drafting, revising, and improving research prose including introductions, discussion sections, and abstracts
  • Statistical code — writing and debugging R, Stata, Mplus, and SPSS code with strong attention to methodological context
  • Structured reasoning — working through analytic decisions, research design questions, and interpretation of findings
  • Document creation — producing formatted reports, codebooks, and documentation from existing content

Gemini is particularly useful for:

  • Google Workspace integration — working directly within Google Docs, Sheets, Slides, and Gmail for in-context drafting and editing
  • Web-connected search — retrieving and summarizing current information from the web alongside AI reasoning
  • Multimodal tasks — analyzing images, figures, and mixed-format documents
  • NotebookLM — Google’s tool powered by Gemini is especially useful for uploading numerous PDFs (e.g., a set of papers for a review) and asking questions across them. Since it references specific information from the documents, this can be very helpful for supporting the review and synthesis of literature.

8.1.2 Consensus

Consensus is an AI-powered academic search engine that retrieves and synthesizes findings directly from peer-reviewed literature. It’s designed specifically for research questions and returns evidence-based answers grounded in specific papers rather than web results. Yale provides free Pro access to all faculty, staff, and students. To activate, sign up at consensus.app using your Yale email address.

Consensus main features:

  • Evidence-based search — results link directly to peer-reviewed sources with citations
  • Consensus meter — summarizes the degree of agreement or disagreement across studies on a given topic
  • Study snapshots — extracts key findings, sample sizes, and methods from individual papers
  • Filters — narrow results by study design, sample type, journal, and year

Consensus is espcially useful for:

  • Scoping a new research area quickly
  • Identifying key papers and research gaps before a systematic search
  • Checking the state of evidence on a specific claim or intervention
  • Supporting manuscript introductions and literature reviews

8.1.3 Grammarly

Grammarly is an AI-powered writing assistant that checks grammar, spelling, punctuation, clarity, and style in real time. The recommended version for CWER team members is the Grammarly desktop app, which works across all the applications you already use like Word, Outlook, email clients, web browsers, and desktop applications, without any copying and pasting. Suggestions appear inline wherever you are writing.

Grammarly is most useful for:

  • Polishing manuscript drafts, reports, and partner communications before sharing
  • Catching typos and grammatical errors in research reports and grant proposals
  • Improving sentence clarity and concision in methods and results sections
  • Checking team member writing during the review process

Do not use Grammarly with text containing sensitive research data or participant information. Grammarly processes text on external servers. For sensitive content, use Microsoft Word’s built-in Editor (Review → Editor) instead, which provides grammar and style checking without sending text to external servers. You can also temporarily turn Grammarly off for a specific application using the Grammarly desktop app settings.

8.1.4 Yale’s Clarity

Yale developed its own platform for accessing existing AI models. It’s called Clarity and since it is designed to protect sensitive information it is the only platform approved for working with identifiable or sensitive research data. Clarity provides secure, encrypted connections and assures that Yale data will not be used to train AI models. It supports Claude, GPT-4o, and Gemini, and is appropriate for most high-risk data except for electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI). If your work involves ePHI or data use agreements with external partners, check with Mike or Joanna before using any AI tool — including Clarity.

When using Claude, Gemini, or any other external AI tool outside of Clarity, never paste real participant data. Use anonymized or synthetic examples instead.

We do not have a blanket recommendation for other tools, though the landscape is evolving quickly. When in doubt, ask Mike or Joanna before using a new AI tool for anything related to lab work.

8.2 General Prompting Principles

Getting useful output from AI requires clear, specific input. A few principles that apply across all tasks:

Give context first. AI tools have no memory of your project unless you tell them (you can do this in settings). Before asking for help, describe what you are working on, what software you are using, what you have already tried, and what the output should look like.

Be specific about what you want. “Help me clean this data” is too vague. Here’s a better prompt: “I need to recode variables coded -99 and -98 to NA, reverse score items 3, 7, and 12, and compute a mean composite score across items 1-10, excluding cases with more than 20% missing. Write R code using tidyverse.” gives the AI something concrete to work with.

Treat output as a first draft. Review AI-generated code before running it and check that it does what you intended after running it. AI output can be subtly wrong in ways that do not provide errors. Always verify results against expectations.

Use AI as a thought partner, not an oracle. Some of the most valuable uses of AI are not asking it to produce something, but asking it to think alongside you. It’s useful for surfacing considerations you may have missed, identifying gaps in a plan, or presenting the argument against an approach you are leaning toward. The measurement development prompt below is a concrete example of this in practice.

Note AI contributions. Document AI assistance in your script headers and, where appropriate, in manuscript methods sections. This reflects our commitment to research transparency described in the Being a Team Member chapter.

8.3 Idea Generation

AI is well-suited for early-stage thinking such as exploring a literature you are less familiar with, stress-testing a research design, or identifying considerations you may have overlooked. The key is to treat AI output as a starting point that you verify and refine, not as a definitive answer.

8.3.1 Examples

Exploring an unfamiliar literature > “I am designing a study on teacher emotional labor in high-poverty schools. What are the key theoretical frameworks and empirical debates I should be aware of before conducting a literature search?”

Stress-testing a research design > “Here is my proposed design for a quasi-experimental study of an SEL intervention. What are the most significant threats to validity I should anticipate, and how might a reviewer challenge my methodological approach?”

Identifying overlooked considerations > “We are developing a survey to measure school climate from the perspective of elementary students. What measurement challenges or practical considerations might we be overlooking?”

Generating hypotheses > “Based on the stress-buffering model of social support, what hypotheses might I generate about the moderating role of teacher-student relationship quality on the effects of adverse childhood experiences on academic outcomes?”

Brainstorming research questions > “We have a longitudinal dataset with annual assessments of social-emotional skills and academic achievement from kindergarten through fifth grade in a high-poverty urban district. What research questions might be theoretically interesting and practically meaningful to pursue?”

Anticipating limitations > “What are the most common limitations reviewers identify in meta-analyses of school-based mental health interventions, and how are they typically addressed in the discussion section?”

8.4 Reviewing & Editing

When asking AI to review written work, the most useful output comes from prompts that specify the review criteria clearly and explicitly discourage flattery. The following system prompt is used for reviewing research writing in CWER. It can be pasted at the start of a conversation or set as a system prompt in a Claude Project.

8.4.1 Examples

System prompt for reviewing research writing:

[Provided as instructions for Claude folder/project]. 
The content in this folder concerns my research writing for 
you to review. When I ask you to review something I've 
written, ensure the following:
1. Text is in APA format unless requested otherwise.
2. The writing is clear, as free of jargon as possible, and concise.
3. Logical arguments are sound.
4. There are no statements that are weakly argued or lack 
   justification. Arguments should not overstate or understate 
   the evidence.
5. If I'm critiquing someone's work, make sure I'm doing so 
   charitably and accurately.

Do not flatter me when reviewing my work. Be constructively 
critical and put yourself in the role of a reviewer at a 
scientific journal who is aiming to improve the work through 
strong critique.

Reviewing parts of your manuscript:

Please review the following [methods / results / discussion] 
section using the criteria in your instructions. Identify the 
most important issues first, then secondary issues. Be specific 
about what to fix and why.

[paste text here]

Verifying a reference:

Please verify that all of my references are accurate and correctly formatted in APA 7th edition style.

[paste references here]

Checking for misreporting:

Here are my analysis results and the corresponding text from my 
results section. Please check for any discrepancies between the 
numbers in the text and the numbers in the output — wrong values, 
rounding errors, or results described in the wrong direction.

Results output: [paste output]
Results text: [paste text]
